The Academic Writing

Lesson 1 — Academic Texts, Structure, and Reading Fields

📚 What is an Academic Text?
Academic Text
Educational texts that use jargons and specialized language. They are formally written and used in academic, scholarly, or professional settings.

Why Read Academic Texts?

  • 1It is a process of working with the text.
  • 2The reader recreates the meaning of the text.
  • 3Readers negotiate the meaning with the author by applying their prior knowledge.
  • 4It improves critical thinking skills.
  • 5It tests students' comprehension levels.
📋 Types of Academic Texts
TypeDescription
TextbooksLearning materials used in courses
Student EssaysWritten arguments or analyses by students
DissertationsExtended research works for academic degrees
Research ArticlesScholarly papers reporting original research
Case StudiesIn-depth analysis of specific cases or examples
ReportsStructured documents presenting research or findings
🔬 Reading in Various Academic Fields

🔬 Science

  • Empirical research
  • Data and experiments
  • Technical vocabulary

📜 History

  • Primary sources
  • Chronological analysis
  • Contextualization

📚 Literature

  • Textual analysis
  • Literary devices
  • Author's intent

➕ Mathematics

  • Logical reasoning
  • Proofs and formulas
  • Symbolic language
🏛️ Structure of Academic Texts (IMRaD)
I
Introduction — Introduces the topic, background, and purpose of the study.
M
Method — Describes the research design and procedures used.
R
Results — Presents the findings or data gathered.
D
Discussion — Interprets and explains the significance of the results.
💡 Tips in Reading Academic Texts
  • 1Search related topics that will make it easier to understand the concepts.
  • 2Read every sentence. DO NOT skip over. Write down words or phrases you don't understand.
  • 3Reflect on what the text is trying to explain and be mindful in interpreting what you read.
  • 4Make sure that what you are reading is a reliable source.
📖 Lesson 2: Skills in Reading Academic Texts

Skills in Reading Academic Texts

Lesson 2 — Clarity, Outlining, Thesis Statements & Text Condensation

✨ Clarity
Clarity
Clarity in academic writing means that the text is written in a clear and organized manner so readers can easily understand and follow the ideas presented.
📋 Outlining
Outline
A structured list divided into headings and subheadings that presents ideas from general to specific, showing how a topic will be developed.

Purpose of an Outline

  • 1Shows main points and supporting points.
  • 2Organizes ideas from general to specific details.
  • 3Shows how a topic will be developed (logical, chronological, problem-solution, thematic).
  • 4Helps readers identify the main idea and thesis of the text.

Two Forms of Outline

Topic Outline
Uses phrase statements.
Sentence Outline
Uses complete sentence statements.

Outline Formats

A. Alphanumeric

  • Roman numerals: I, II, III
  • Capital letters: A, B, C
  • Arabic numerals: 1, 2, 3
  • Lowercase letters: a, b, c
  • Parenthetical: (1), (2)

B. Numeric

  • 1. Major heading
  • 1.1 Sub-heading
  • 1.2 Sub-heading
  • 1.2.1 Minor point
  • 2. Next major heading
🎯 Thesis Statement
Thesis Statement
A short statement — usually one sentence — that summarizes the main point or claim of an essay, research paper, or other academic writing. It identifies the main idea and/or central purpose of the text.

Tips in Writing a Thesis Statement

  • 1Be clear and concise. Simplicity is the key.
  • 2Do not say too much — include only what is important.
  • 3Avoid technical jargons unless you are confident your reader will understand them.
  • 4Do not take sides or inject personal opinion into the thesis statement.
📝 Text Condensation Skills
SkillDescription
ParaphraseRephrasing the text according to how you understand it; stating texts in your own words. Not constrained by word length.
PrécisSumming up an academic or official document by paraphrasing academically. Condenses a text to about one-fifth or one-sixth of its original length without omitting essential details.
SummaryRestates only the author's main ideas, omitting extra examples and evidence. Most condensed form; reflective of the main idea.
AbstractSummarizes the major aspects of the entire paper in one paragraph of 300 words or less, in a prescribed sequence.

An Abstract Primarily Includes:

  • 1The overall purpose of the study and the research problem(s).
  • 2The basic design of the study.
  • 3Major findings or trends found as a result of the analysis.
  • 4Brief summary of interpretations and conclusions.

Reaction Paper

Lesson 3 — Writing, Structure, Principles & Tips

💬 What is a Reaction Paper?
Reaction Paper
An essay in which you describe the emotions a certain person, object, or event caused in you. It is not only an expression of your emotion — it is a type of academic paper that requires the ability to present thoughts and ideas in a scholarly manner.
Writing a reaction paper typically involves providing a commentary on a specific article, journal, or book. To do this effectively, you must remain critical and develop your thoughts while reading.
Note: The reaction paper's content should be interesting and thought-provoking. This means moving beyond simply saying "I liked it" or "I didn't like it" — it requires you to engage with the material in a deeper and critical way.

When Do We Write a Reaction Paper?

🎬 Creative Works

  • Films & Arts
  • Literature
  • Drama

🎓 Academic

  • Books
  • Research Articles

📱 Media Content

  • News
  • Feature Stories
  • Social media content and exchanges

Things to Consider Before Writing

Identification
Pinpoint specific points you wish to argue, discuss, or expand upon. This shall be your thesis statement.
Stand
You may agree and add insights, or completely disagree by explaining why an author's point is flawed. This forms the body of your reaction paper.
🎯 5 Purposes of Writing a Reaction Paper
PurposeDescription
Professional Self-ExpressionHelps students develop the ability to express their thoughts in an academic and professional manner.
Focused InsightAllows students to explore one specific aspect of a larger subject. By focusing on a narrow case study, students gain deeper insight into the broader topic.
Skill DemonstrationServes as a platform for students to demonstrate three essential pillars of education: the ability to research, understand, and communicate information.
Demonstration of MasteryProvides an opportunity for students to show they have mastered the course material and can exercise their verbal and written skills effectively.
Personal InputUnlike purely objective reports, a reaction paper encourages students to have personal input and share their own opinions in the conclusion.
📋 Parts of a Reaction Paper
1
Introduction — Briefly introduce the material (title and author). Highlight main points. Present a definitive statement summarizing your reaction and core argument.
2
Body — Lead with a topic sentence → Provide evidence → Analyze the connection → Conclude with evaluation.
3
Conclusion — Restate your thesis → Summarize key points → Provide final thoughts.
4
References — Cite every source. Prioritize scholarly evidence. Provide reliable references. Respect intellectual property.
⚖️ 5 Principles on Writing a Good Reaction Paper
PrincipleDescription
Objectivity & BalanceA good reaction paper touches on both a work's strengths and weaknesses, as well as its advantages and disadvantages.
ComprehensivenessWhile you should consult multiple resources to support your claims, you are also expected to remain concise and focused in your reaction paper.
ConstructivenessIdentifying weaknesses is equally important as strengths. Since weaknesses highlight areas for improvement, you should offer constructive points for a more favorable outcome instead of simply labeling a work as "bad."
Conciseness & ClarityReaction paper should be clearly constructed. Avoid unnecessary phrases, especially when content is focused on evaluating works.
Correctness & CompletenessFollow the prescribed formats and present correct information.
✍️ 6 Tips in Writing a Reaction Paper
TipHow to Do It
PreparationBegin by creating a detailed outline. This helps you identify your various reactions, organize supporting evidence (justifications), and ensures the paper stays on the right track.
Read & AnalyzeRead the material in a deep and analytical manner. Identify a major point or thesis to center your paper around. Take notes during reading — this provides a reliable reference when you begin drafting.
IntroductionStart with a summary of the work that is at least three sentences long. It must include the title and author, ending with a strong thesis statement at the conclusion of the paragraph.
Body ParagraphsFocus each paragraph on a single, concise reaction. State your point clearly and follow it with supporting reasons or observations. Use personal indicators like "I believe..." or "In my opinion..." to distinguish your voice.
ConclusionWrap up by restating your thesis and summarizing your main reactions. To leave a lasting impact, consider ending with a challenge to the readers.
ContextualizeIncorporate at least one real-life example to make it easier to explain and justify your stance.
🎬 Guide Questions When Reviewing a Film
ElementQuestions to Ask
PlotIs the sequence of events logical and entertaining? Are there twists and turns? What storytelling techniques were used?
ActingDid the actors stay in character? Was their performance compelling? Did they project their voices well?
CinematographyDo the shots contribute to the overall mood or theme the director wishes to convey?
Musical ScoreDoes the background music enhance the film's atmosphere?
Special EffectsWere there special effects in the movie? Were they appropriate and aesthetically pleasing?
EditingWere the scenes logically arranged? Were there any unnecessary scenes?
ScriptWere the lines natural for the characters and context? Is the dialogue original, or is it filled with clichés?

Critique Paper

Lesson 4 — Critical Approaches, Characteristics & Writing Techniques

🔍 What is a Critique Paper?
Critique Paper
An evaluative analysis that carefully examines another writer's work through either a focused lens (specific literary techniques) or a holistic assessment. The goal is to provide a balanced, in-depth critique that highlights both strengths and weaknesses.

Benefits of Writing a Critique

  • 1Knowledge of the work's subject area or related works.
  • 2An understanding of the work's purpose, intended audience, and development of argument.
  • 3A recognition of the strengths and weaknesses of the work.
✅ 8 Characteristics of a Good Critique
CharacteristicMeaning
ComprehensiveAnalyzes all the elements of the genre to which the particular work belongs.
CriticalThe review is in-depth. Critical approaches to writing a review are employed by the author.
TimelyThe review is about a work that is relatively recent.
ObjectiveThe review is not tainted by the author's personal biases and prejudices.
CredibleAcceptable to many, if not all. The reviewer uses generally accepted criteria in evaluating the subject work.
OriginalNot merely piecing together other critics' reviews. The author provides their own independent inputs.
FairThe author does not unfairly compare the work with another work.
DecisiveIt decides whether the work is good or bad, of quality or poor quality.

How to Be a Good Critic

  • 1Knowledgeable of the genre and the topic of the text.
  • 2Proficient in determining the strengths and weaknesses of the text.
  • 3Truthful and not easily influenced by others' opinions.
🔬 6 Critical Approaches in Writing a Critique
Various critical approaches govern the writing of a critique paper. Just as a theoretical framework is essential to research, a critical approach is fundamental to a critique — it grounds the critique and answers the question: What principles did the writer use?

1. Formalist Approach

Asserts that the meaning of a work is found entirely within the text itself, treating it as a distinct, self-contained piece of art. Rejects external factors (author's life, historical context). Focuses on structure, style, and content.

Key aspects:

  • a. Author's techniques in resolving contradictions within the work
  • b. Central passage that sums up the entirety of the work
  • c. Contribution of parts and the work as a whole to its aesthetic quality
  • d. Relationship of the form and the content
  • e. Use of imagery to develop the symbols in the work
  • f. Interconnectedness of various parts of the work
  • g. Unity in the works

2. Feminist Approach

Examines literature through the lens of gender dynamics, focusing on the portrayal of women as subjects of sociopolitical, psychological, and economic oppression. Challenges patriarchal norms and explores how meaning is constructed based on sex-ascribed roles.

Key aspects:

  • a. Shows that culture determines gender
  • b. Shows gender equality (or lack of it) as presented in text
  • c. Shows gender issues in literary works and other aspects of daily life
  • d. Shows women are socially, politically, psychologically, and economically oppressed by patriarchy
  • e. Shows patriarchal ideology as an overpowering presence

Waves of Feminism

1st Wave (early 1900s)
Defined by the suffragette movement; primary goal was securing women's right to vote for political equality.
2nd Wave (1960s–1980s)
Targeted systemic legal barriers; focused on reproductive freedom and equality in the professional workplace.
3rd Wave (1990s–2010s)
Broadened scope by introducing intersectionality — the study of how different social identities overlap — as a core principle.
4th Wave (2010–present)
Maintains goals of previous waves while placing significant emphasis on combating sexual violence and harassment against women.

3. Reader-Response Approach

Asserts that a text remains incomplete until it is engaged by an audience. Readers are active participants who bring their unique backgrounds and perspectives to the material, essentially 'creating' the meaning. A text does not have a single, fixed message.

Key aspects:

  • a. Interaction between the reader and the text in creating meaning
  • b. The impact of the reader's delivery of sounds and visuals on enhancing and changing meaning

4. Biographical Approach

Premised on the fact that the author's life has a significant bearing on how the work is written. Circumstances including education, background, and way of life are reflected in the writing. The author brings their entire belief system into the work.

Key aspects:

  • a. Autobiographical elements such as real-life experiences, people, or places in the story
  • b. The author's emotional state, desires, unfulfilled wishes, or psychological conflicts manifest in the text
  • c. The author's political, religious, or philosophical stances portrayed directly or through characters
  • d. Influence of parents, siblings, spouses, or significant others on relationships depicted in the work

5. Marxist Criticism

Concerned with differences between economic classes and implications of a capitalist system, such as conflicts between the working class and the elite. Attempts to reveal that the ultimate source of people's experience is the socioeconomic system.

Key aspects:

  • a. Social class as represented in the work
  • b. Social class of the writer/creator
  • c. Social class of the characters
  • d. Conflicts and interactions between economic classes

6. Historical Approach

Recognizes that social and cultural environments have a tremendous effect on events and situations. Requires consideration of time, social milieu, political climate, and socio-political context. The work is influenced by everything around it.

Key aspects:

  • a. Chronology: placing events in order to understand cause-and-effect
  • b. Change over Time: identifying how societal norms, laws, and cultural attitudes shift
  • c. Contextualization: connecting a specific text or event to the broader 'big picture' of the era
  • d. Contingency: recognizing that historical outcomes were not inevitable; they were shaped by specific human choices and chance occurrences
🗣️ Techniques in Offering Praise or Suggesting Improvements
When writing a critique, use these language techniques to frame evaluations professionally and with appropriate certainty.
TechniquePurposeExample
1. Qualifying adjectives & adverbsIdentifies how many or how much is affected by a view."Some users find the new interface difficult to navigate compared with the first."
2. Modal verbsAttaches appropriate extent of certainty to a claim."Some users may find the new interface difficult to navigate compared with the first."
3. Tentative verbsAssigns extent of certainty to a claim; similar to modal verbs."It appears that users find the new interface difficult to navigate compared with the first."
4. Offering an ideal stateExpresses what could have made the work better or what could improve it."What the researcher could have done was to summarize the data in a line graph, not a pie chart, since it dealt with trends over a 30-year period."

3 Steps in Writing a Critique Paper

1
Decide what to look at. Determine which aspects of the item you are going to evaluate. Ask: "What is it that can be good or bad about something I'm going to review?" (e.g., when watching a movie: acting, writing, cinematography)
2
Decide what makes things good or bad. Figure out what you mean by 'good' and 'bad.' Apply the standards you've set to the material based on your preferences and the critical approach you've chosen.
3
Write your review or critique. Begin putting your essay together, incorporating your evaluation using the critical approach and language techniques discussed.
⚖️ Reaction Paper vs. Critique Paper
FeatureReaction PaperCritique Paper
Primary FocusPersonal feelings and experiencesAcademic merit and technical quality
ToneSubjective, informal, and emotionalObjective, formal, and analytical
EvidencePersonal anecdotes and "I" statementsEvidence from texts and external logic; third POV
GoalTo process how you felt about the workTo evaluate academic merit and technical quality
Key Question"How did I feel about this, and why?""What principles did the writer use, and how effectively?"
ExampleWriting about how a documentary made you feel guilty about your plastic use.Analyzing that same documentary by pointing out biased interviewing techniques or lack of peer-reviewed data.

Citation Styles

Lesson 5 — Plagiarism, APA, MLA, IEEE, AMA & Chicago

⚠️ Plagiarism & Citation
Plagiarism
The act of taking someone else's work, ideas, or intellectual property and presenting them as your own. In academic and professional environments, this is classified as academic misconduct — associated with labels like "stealing," "piracy," and "copyright infringement."
Citation
A formal acknowledgment that specific information in your work originates from an external source. It provides readers with the essential metadata required to locate the original material, including the author's name, title of the work, and publication details such as company, date, and page numbers.
📚 5 Citation Styles & Their Disciplines
While citation styles vary across academic disciplines, they all share the common goal of organizing source information within the text and the final reference list.
Citation StyleAcademic Discipline
APAPsychology, Education, Hotel and Restaurant Management, Business, Economics, and other Social Sciences
MLALiterature, Arts, and Humanities
IEEEEngineering
AMAMedicine, Health, and Natural Sciences
ChicagoReference books, non-academic, periodicals (e.g. newspapers, magazines, journals)
📝 APA Citation Format
APA Formula: "Who, When, What, and Where" — requires the author's name, publication date, title, and source location. Sources are credited using in-text parenthetical citations that correspond to a final References list. A supplemental Bibliography page lists every resource consulted, even those not directly quoted.

APA Reference List Rules

RuleDetails
ListingArrange alphabetically by author's surname, followed by initials.
One author, several worksArrange by year of publication, starting with the most recent.
Several authors, one publicationIf more than seven authors, list the first six, then an ellipsis (…), then the last author's name (no ampersand).
SpacingDouble-space all entries.
CapitalizationCapitalize proper names and the first word in each title, and the first word after a colon or dash.
IndentationUse hanging indent — flush left for the first line, then indent second and succeeding lines five spaces from the left.

APA In-Text Citation

APA in-text citations follow an author-date-page sequence. Items in parentheses are separated by commas. Page numbers are preceded by "p." (single page) or "pp." (multiple pages).

Example 1: Marquez (2016, p. 35) cites the advantage of e-books over traditional books.

Example 2: Marquez (2016) cites the advantage of e-books over traditional books, claiming that "portability and manipulability are key features of e-books" (p. 35).

Electronic Sources (APA)

APA follows a "who, what, when, and where" structure for electronic sources. When a DOI (Digital Object Identifier) is available, it serves as the complete retrieval record — no URL or retrieval date is needed. When no DOI exists, include the URL preceded by "Retrieved from," along with the document title, publication date (or "n.d." if unavailable), and author name. Sources without an author are alphabetized by title.

DOI Available

  • DOI serves as the complete retrieval record
  • No URL or retrieval date needed

No DOI

  • Include URL preceded by "Retrieved from"
  • Include document title, publication date (or "n.d." if unavailable)
  • Include author name; sources without author alphabetized by title
📝 MLA Citation Format
MLA (Modern Language Association) style is the standard citation format for Literature, Arts, and Humanities. Unlike APA, it requires the author's full name (not just initials) and follows a "who, what, where, and when" sequence. All borrowed materials must be documented in a final list titled Works Cited.

MLA Works Cited Rules

RuleDetails
ListingAuthor's surname appears first.
One author, several worksAlphabetize the works; use three hyphens (---) to signify the author's name for every work after the first.
Several authors, one publicationUse the surname of the first author, then continue listing the rest of the authors' names.
Works with no authorsUse the title and alphabetize. Titles of books, magazines, journals, films, and newspapers should be italicized.
SpacingDouble-space the Works Cited list.
CapitalizationEach word except conjunctions (and, by) and articles (a, an, the) should be capitalized.
IndentationUse the hanging indent.

MLA In-Text Citation

MLA in-text citations follow the author's name and page number format. The word "page" may be omitted; a comma is NOT used to separate the information.

Example 1: Only 15 percent of the respondents prefer traditional books (Marquez 13).

Example 2: According to Marquez, only 15 percent of the respondents prefer traditional books (13).

⚖️ APA vs. MLA
FeatureAPAMLA
Title for reference listing"References""Works Cited"
CapitalizationOnly the first letter of the title of the work is capitalized.Proper names, first word of title, and first word after a colon or semicolon are capitalized.
AbstractRequiredNot required
Headings in Main BodyHas headings to indicate important sections (e.g. "Method" or "Results")No such headings.
