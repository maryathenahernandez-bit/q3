The Entrepreneur

Lesson 1 — Humanities 5: Entrepreneurship

📖 Key Definitions
Entrepreneur
Someone who starts and manages a business, undertaking the risks associated with it. The word comes from the French entreprendre meaning "to undertake" — coined by Jean Baptiste Say.
Entrepreneurship
The dynamic process of conceptualizing, financing, and running a business enterprise to provide goods and services and earn profit.
Creative Destruction
How new innovations and businesses replace old ones, leading to progress but also disrupting existing industries. Term coined by Joseph A. Schumpeter.
Innovation
The development of new ideas, processes, products, or services that create value.
👨‍🏫 Definitions by Scholars
ScholarDefinition / Key Idea
Arthur H. Cole
Economic Historian		Entrepreneurship is a purposeful activity or integrated sequence of decisions to initiate, organize, or expand a profit-oriented business unit for producing or distributing economic goods or services.
Peter F. Drucker
Father of Modern Management		What successful entrepreneurs have in common is not a certain kind of personality, but a commitment to the systematic practice of innovation. Entrepreneurship can be learned and practiced.
Joseph A. SchumpeterAssociated entrepreneurship with innovation. Introduced the concept of Creative Destruction — new innovations replacing old ones, leading to progress while disrupting existing industries.
⚡ 5 Forms of Innovation (Schumpeter)
  • 1Launching of a new product or service
  • 2Development of a new production method
  • 3Opening of a new market
  • 4Discovery of a new source of supply of raw materials
  • 5Carrying out a new organization of industry
🏛️ 5 Fundamental Principles of Entrepreneurship
  • 1Having a vision
  • 2Providing innovative products or services
  • 3Managing effectively
  • 4Taking risks and opportunities
  • 5Making informed decisions
🏆 10 Personal Entrepreneur Competencies (PECs)

🥇 Achievement Cluster

The drive and motivation of an entrepreneur to set and accomplish business goals.

  • Opportunity Seeking & Initiative
  • Persistence
  • Fulfilling Commitments
  • Demand for Quality & Efficiency
  • Taking Calculated Risk

📋 Planning Cluster

Setting clear goals, gathering information, and creating organized plans for business decisions.

  • Goal Setting
  • Information Seeking
  • Systematic Planning & Monitoring

💪 Power Cluster

The entrepreneur's ability to influence, lead, and build relationships that support business success.

  • Persuasion & Networking
  • Independence & Self-Confidence

The Entrepreneurial Mindset

Lesson 1.2 — Humanities 5: Entrepreneurship

🎨 5 Learning Styles & Skill Sets
There are five learning styles that shape how entrepreneurs think and act.
StyleDescriptionChallenges
ImaginingPrefers concrete experiences; uses varying thinking processes to generate multiple solutions and ideas.May struggle with execution and follow-through; can be overwhelmed by too many ideas.
AnalyzingPrefers abstract information through reflection; enjoys logical problem solving and theory formulation.Can overanalyze; may struggle in fast-paced environments; hard to embrace uncertainty.
DecidingDrawn to application of theory through practical, task-oriented problem solving.May overlook deeper analysis; can be impatient; might take risks without full consideration.
InitiatingInterested in practical activities; relies on intuition; learns from social interactions.May take risks without enough preparation; can struggle with long-term planning.
BalancedFlexible style that allows adaptation on a situational basis; combines all four styles.May take longer to decide; can experience decision fatigue; harder to form a distinct identity.
🔍 Opportunity Seeking, Screening & Seizing
Opportunity
An idea that has the potential to be developed into a business venture.

A. Sources of Opportunity

Sources of opportunity are divided into Internal and External:

SourceTypeDescriptionExample
The UnexpectedInternalSuccesses, failures, and outside events that were not anticipated can open new opportunities.3M's accidental invention of Post-it Notes
IncongruitiesInternalA gap between what is and what should be (inefficiencies or customer dissatisfaction) can lead to innovation.Netflix replacing Blockbuster by removing late fees
Process NeedsInternalIdentifying a weak or missing link in a process to improve efficiency.Amazon's One-Click Checkout reducing cart abandonment
Industry & Market ChangesExternalShifts in industry structure, trends, or consumer preferences create opportunities.COVID-19 accelerating demand for remote work tools
Demographic ChangesExternalPopulation trends such as aging, urbanization, or income shifts.Aging population increasing demand for elderly care & medical tech
Changes in PerceptionExternalEvolving consumer values and cultural shifts.Demand for sustainable products fueling eco-fashion
New KnowledgeExternalAdvances in science, technology, or business models.AI advancements leading to new businesses like ChatGPT, Tesla self-driving

B. How to Spot Opportunities

  • 1Listen to the Market
  • 2Feel the Competition
  • 3Look at the Trends

C. Opportunity Screening

Opportunity Screening
Entrepreneurs evaluate the market, competitors, and industry trends to assess whether an opportunity is worth pursuing.

An opportunity should:

  • 1Bring superior value to customers
  • 2Provide a solution to a compelling problem
  • 3Fully satisfy a need or want
  • 4Be a potential cash cow (financially viable)
  • 5Match the skills and resources of the entrepreneur

The 12 R's of Opportunity Screening

CriterionDescription
RelevanceDoes the opportunity align with the company's vision, mission, and goals?
ResonanceThe opportunity must resonate with the values the entrepreneur wishes to impart.
ReinforcementDoes it resonate with the entrepreneur's interest, preference, or taste?
RevenuesMust determine the sales potential of the products and services.
ResponsivenessCan the business quickly adapt to market changes and trends?
ReachGood chances of expanding through branches, distributorships, dealerships, and franchise outlets.
RangeDoes the opportunity allow for expansion into other markets or product lines?
Revolutionary ImpactIs it the next big thing? Is it a game changer?
ReturnsWhat are the expected financial and non-financial gains?
Relative Ease of ImplementationIs it easy to implement?
Resources RequiredWhat capital, technology, and manpower are needed?
RisksWhat are the potential challenges, competition, and threats?

D. Opportunity Seizing

Opportunity Seizing
Recognizing and taking advantage of favorable situations or chances when they arise. Involves determining critical factors that could affect the outcome of the chosen opportunity.
Positioning Statement
A concise explanation of how an entrepreneur wants the market to perceive his/her brand. It includes a brief explanation of the product or service and how it addresses a need of the target market.

Characteristics of a good Positioning Statement:

  • 1Simple, memorable, credible, and tailored to the target market
  • 2Provides an easily understood unique description of the brand
  • 3Helps evaluate the consistency of marketing efforts with the brand
  • 4Leaves room for growth and development
💡 Product Conceptualization
Product Conceptualization
The phase during which entrepreneurs write their ideas on paper (concept statement) to identify concepts that will lay the foundation of their business model.

How to Make a Concept Statement

  • 1Create a concept similar to successful products and ride market trends.
  • 2Find a market niche or segment not yet filled by competitors.
  • 3Conceptualize a product or service where competition is weak.

Prototypes

🗒️ Low-Fidelity Prototype

  • Easy and quick to make
  • Usually paper-based
  • Gives a preview without testing

🖥️ High-Fidelity Prototype

  • Looks similar to the real product
  • Interactive
  • Offers insightful feedback from testers

4 Key Features of a Prototype

  • 1Representation
  • 2Precision
  • 3Interactivity
  • 4Evolution
🔄 5-Phase Enterprise Life Cycle
PhaseNameKey Focus
Phase 1DevelopmentThe entrepreneur determines if the opportunity is worth investing by gathering market information and evaluating skills and resources.
Phase 2Start-upThe business concept is tested and adjusted to meet end-user expectations until there are sufficiently compelling reasons to officially launch.
Phase 3GrowthEmphasis shifts from planning to execution. The business should be generating profits and moving toward sustainable performance.
Phase 4ExpansionThe business broadens its network, customer base, and offerings through new territories or mergers and acquisitions.
Phase 5MaturityEntrepreneurs face two options — push for further expansion, or exit the business.

Successful Filipino Entrepreneurship

Lesson 1.3 — Humanities 5: Entrepreneurship

🌐 Key Concepts
Entrepreneurial Ecosystem
The community and support system around entrepreneurs — including funds, technology, human resources, and professional services — in a business environment where government policies encourage and safeguard them.
Serial Entrepreneurship
The practice of starting several businesses one after another over a period of time. A serial entrepreneur starts, runs, exits, and then starts again.

Components of an Entrepreneurial Ecosystem

  • 1Entrepreneurs — people with business ideas
  • 2Investors — people who provide funding
  • 3Universities & Schools — education and research
  • 4Government — rules, policies, and support programs
  • 5Mentors & Networks — guidance and connections
  • 6Banks & Financial Institutions
  • 7Service Providers — lawyers, accountants, consultants
Philippine Example: Metro Manila (especially Makati, BGC, and Quezon City) is the center of the Philippine entrepreneurial ecosystem, supported by universities such as UP Diliman, Ateneo de Manila, and De La Salle University.

🏆 Notable Filipino Entrepreneurs

Study each entrepreneur's background, key business contributions, and the industries they transformed.

1. Henry Sy (1924 – 2019)

SM Malls & BDO Founder
  • Started ShoeMart (SM) at age 12 — a small shoe store in Manila (1958) that became the Philippines' largest mall chain
  • SM Prime Holdings operates 70+ malls in the Philippines and several in China
  • Topped Forbes Philippines' 50 Richest list for 11 consecutive years (2008–2018)
  • Net worth estimated at $20 billion; ranked 53rd wealthiest person in the world
  • Controlled Banco de Oro (BDO) — the biggest bank in the Philippines by assets
  • Founded National University (NU); known for his humble and frugal lifestyle

2. Tony Tan Caktiong (Born January 5, 1953)

Jollibee / JFC Founder
  • Founded Jollibee in 1978 — started as an ice cream parlor in 1975, pivoted to burgers, spaghetti, and fried chicken
  • Jollibee Foods Corporation (JFC) now owns 6,500+ stores worldwide
  • JFC brands: Chowking, Greenwich, Red Ribbon, Mang Inasal, The Coffee Bean & Tea Leaf
  • Also owns a majority stake in DoubleDragon Properties
  • Net worth over $2 billion; key lesson: adapt to change and innovate

3. Lucio Tan (Born July 17, 1934)

Fortune Tobacco, PAL, PNB, Tanduay Rum
  • Born in Fujian, China; worked as a janitor while studying chemical engineering
  • Founded Fortune Tobacco (1960s) — became the biggest cigarette manufacturer in the Philippines
  • Established Asia Breweries (1970s) to challenge San Miguel Beer
  • Acquired Philippine Airlines (PAL) in 1995, saving it from financial trouble
  • Owns Philippine National Bank (PNB) — merged with Allied Bank in 2013
  • Tanduay Distillers produces Tanduay Rum, one of the world's best-selling rums; also owns Eton Properties and University of the East (UE)

4. Socorro Ramos (Born September 23, 1923)

National Book Store Founder
  • Worked as a salesperson at Goodwill Bookstore at age 18
  • In 1940, put up a small stall on Escolta Street, Manila selling school supplies
  • Secretly sold banned imported books during the Japanese occupation in WWII
  • Rebuilt after a typhoon in 1948 destroyed her store by borrowing money
  • National Book Store grew into the largest bookstore chain in the Philippines (230+ branches)
  • Founded Anvil Publishing to make books more affordable for Filipinos

5. Mariano Que (1921 – 2017)

Mercury Drug Founder
  • Started Mercury Drug on March 1, 1945 on Bambang Street, Manila with only PHP 100 capital
  • Sold sulfiathiazole (a 'miracle' antibacterial drug) piece by piece from a wooden pushcart
  • Named after Mercury, the Roman god of commerce, speed, and medicine
  • Pioneer of 24/7 pharmacy service and self-service medicine counters in the Philippines
  • Mercury Drug is now the biggest drugstore chain in the Philippines with 1,200+ branches

6. Ramon Ang (Born January 14, 1954)

San Miguel Corporation Chairman
  • Born to a modest ethnic Chinese family; began career selling used car and truck parts
  • Started business career in the late 1970s importing cars and engines
  • Chairman of San Miguel Corporation (SMC); expanded it from beer & food into power, fuel, infrastructure, and telecoms
  • Led SMC's acquisition of Petron Corporation — the largest oil company in the Philippines
  • Founding chair of Eagle Cement Corporation; net worth estimated over $3 billion

7. John Gokongwei (August 11, 1926 – 2019)

URC, Cebu Pacific, Robinsons, JG Summit
  • Started trading goods in Cebu at age 15 after his father passed away (sold peanuts, soap, thread)
  • Founded Universal Robina Corporation (URC) in 1957 — Jack 'n Jill snacks (Piattos, Chippy) and C2 Green Tea
  • Launched Cebu Pacific in 1996 — now the largest budget airline in the Philippines
  • Founded Robinsons Retail Holdings: Robinsons Malls, supermarkets, and department stores
  • Co-founded Digitel (Sun Cellular) — introduced unlimited call & text promos in the Philippines
  • Third richest person in the Philippines when he passed away in 2019 at age 93; net worth over $5 billion

8. Dr. Vicky Belo (Born January 25, 1956)

Belo Medical Group Founder
  • Started her dermatology business in a 44 sq.m. clinic in 1990
  • Founder and medical director of Belo Medical Group — a leading cosmetic surgery clinic in the Philippines
  • Introduced modern liposuction techniques in the Philippines
  • First Filipino member of the American Academy of Cosmetic Surgery (AACS)

9. Josh Mojica (Born July 5, 2004)

Kangkong Chips Original Founder
  • Founder of Kangkong Chips Original — started in 2021 at age 17
  • Based his product on his aunt's kangkong (water spinach) chips recipe
  • Started with only PHP 3,000 initial capital
  • Earned over PHP 100,000 monthly within six months; earned his first million within one year
  • By 2022: two-story factory, 100 employees, 6 flavors, local and international customers
  • Leveraged social media effectively to grow the brand

10. Louise Mabulo (Born 1998)

The Cacao Project Founder — Social Entrepreneur
  • Finalist in Junior MasterChef Philippines in 2011 at just 12 years old; known as the 'Young Chef of the Philippines'
  • Founded The Cacao Project in 2016 to help farmers in Camarines Sur recover from Typhoon Nina
  • Recognized by the UN Environment Programme for sustainable agriculture work
  • Included in Forbes 30 Under 30 Asia for Social Entrepreneurship in 2020
  • Vocal advocate for fair trade and sustainable food systems
